Occupational Therapy for Children with Developmental Delays

What is Occupational Therapy (OT)?

Occupational Therapy helps children develop the skills they need for everyday life—playing, learning, socializing, and becoming independent. It focuses on improving motor skills, sensory processing, attention, and daily activities like eating, dressing, and writing.

Our Approach

Our approach blends play-based activities with evidence-driven techniques and close family involvement to create meaningful, measurable progress.

  • Child-centered and play-based therapy
  • Evidence-based techniques
  • Individualized care plans
  • Family-focused support and home programs

Why Do Children Need Occupational Therapy?

Children with developmental delays may struggle with:

  • Fine motor skills (holding a pencil, buttoning clothes)
  • Gross motor coordination (balance, jumping, running)
  • Sensory processing (over- or under-reacting to sounds, touch, movement)
  • Attention and focus
  • Social interaction and play skills
  • Daily self-care activities

Who Can Benefit?

Occupational Therapy supports children with:

  • Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)
  • ADHD
  • Global developmental delay
  • Sensory Processing Disorder
  • Learning difficulties
  • Down syndrome and other genetic conditions
  • Delayed milestones (speech, motor, social)

Assessment: Understanding Your Child’s Needs

A personalized therapy plan starts with a detailed assessment:

  • Developmental history and parent concerns
  • Standardized assessments and observations
  • Sensory profile evaluation
  • Motor skill and coordination testing
  • Functional skills (daily routines, play, school readiness)

Therapy Plan: How It Works

Each child receives an individualized plan based on their needs.

Key Components:

  • Goal setting (short-term and long-term)
  • Sensory integration activities
  • Fine and gross motor skill training
  • Handwriting and pre-academic skills
  • Self-care training (feeding, dressing, toileting)
  • Social and play skill development

Session Structure:

Typically 30-45 minutes, frequency depends on individual needs.

Parent Involvement

Home programs, guidance for daily routines, and regular feedback sessions.

Benefits of Occupational Therapy

  • Improved independence in daily activities
  • Better focus and attention
  • Enhanced motor coordination and strength
  • Improved sensory regulation
  • Increased confidence and social participation
  • School readiness and improved academic performance

Why Early Intervention Matters

The earlier therapy begins, the better the outcomes.
